# Research summary 23.04.2026
Respondent: girl, aged 12.5, 6th grader in primary school, Warsaw, Poland


1. The student manages a moderate homework load independently Agata handles 2–3+ assignments per week and reports managing them "somehow" (jakoś sobie radzę), suggesting she copes but may not have a fully optimized system.

2. She uses analog tools for planning (calendar + paper) Agata plans her study schedule using a calendar and written notes to track what to do each day, indicating a preference or habit for physical planning methods.

3. She prioritizes by manually mapping out daily tasks She decides what to study each day by laying out her work herself, rather than relying on a digital system to surface priorities automatically.

4. She quickly identifies the "To Do" vs. "Can Wait" distinction When viewing the app, Agata immediately understood that "to do" items are more important and others can be postponed, showing she thinks in terms of urgency/priority.

5. She interprets difficulty as teacher-determined, not self-assessed Agata assumes difficulty ratings (easy/medium/hard) are set by teachers, not students. This reveals an expectation that authority figures define task complexity.

6. She uses visual cues to infer meaning (map icons = difficulty) She figured out that more map icons mean harder tasks, showing she can decode visual patterns even without explicit labels.

7. She recognizes task metadata but isn't sure how to use it Agata identified fields like "status," "subject," and "date" but hesitated when explaining their purpose, suggesting the interface needs clearer affordances.

8. She struggles with the app's search/filter functionality When asked to find a specific history exam, Agata tried using the status filter but didn't successfully locate the task, indicating navigation friction.

9. She expects the app to show chronological + priority-based ordering Agata interpreted the task list as organized by both timing (today vs. tomorrow) and importance, which aligns with how she manually plans.
